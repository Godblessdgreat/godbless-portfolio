# A boutique hotel concept, designed and built in a single sprint.

## The Brief

Mayoral Hotels & Suites started as a speculative project — a concept site built for a Lagos hospitality brand I had been in early conversations with. The acquisition didn't move forward, but the site itself became a portfolio piece worth keeping: a chance to design and ship a full hospitality website end-to-end in under a week using modern AI tools.

The goal: a boutique hotel site that felt premium without leaning on the tired "luxury hotel" visual clichés — no oversized stock photography, no generic "book your stay" hero, no dropdown date pickers anchored in 2014.

## My Role

Solo designer and developer. I owned positioning, copy, visual direction, code, and deployment. Built with HTML, CSS, and progressively layered JavaScript — a deliberately lean stack to test how fast a polished hospitality site could ship without React or a CMS.

## Key Decisions

**Tone over template.** Most hospitality sites read like brochures. I wrote the copy with the voice of a confident host — short sentences, specific details, no marketing fluff. The goal was for prospects to feel they were reading about a real place run by real people, not a templated booking funnel.

**Lean stack, AI-assisted.** No React, no CMS, no booking engine. Pure HTML, CSS, and vanilla JavaScript — generated and refined with Claude and Lovable in a tight feedback loop. The constraint forced discipline: every section had to earn its place, every animation had to be small enough to ship without a library.

**Editorial type, conservative palette.** Hospitality brands usually default to warm metallics and amber lighting. I chose a quieter, more editorial palette — letting typography and spacing carry the premium feel instead of decorative flourishes. The result reads more "boutique residence" than "five-star chain."

**Structured for a real hotel, even as a concept.** The site is built with the structural pieces a real hospitality brand needs — suites/rooms section, services, location, FAQ, enquiry form — so it could be handed over to a hotel team and operated with minimal changes. Concept work that's only a hero and a logo isn't useful evidence of skill.

## What Shipped

A multi-section single-page site covering: hero, story, suites, services, location, FAQ, and an enquiry form. Built with vanilla HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Hosted on Vercel.

The site includes responsive design, smooth scroll, scroll-triggered reveals, a working image gallery, and a client-side enquiry form ready to wire to a real backend.

## Outcomes

As a concept project, the outcome is the case study itself — a complete, deployable hospitality site built end-to-end in a single sprint, demonstrating speed without sacrificing craft.

The project also became a working template I can adapt for real hospitality clients in significantly less time than building from scratch.

## What I'd Do Differently

The site uses placeholder hospitality photography. With a real client engagement, I'd plan a small photo sprint as part of the project scope — even three or four custom shots dramatically outperform stock imagery in this category.

I'd also build in a real booking integration — Cloudbeds, Mews, or a custom Stripe flow — instead of stopping at an enquiry form. Concept sites are forgiven for stopping there; real hotels need the full transaction loop.
